Brief Description of the Site

The Dome of the Rock (Qubbat al-Sakhra) is a famous Islamic shrine located on the Temple Mount in the Old City of Jerusalem. It is the oldest extant example of Islamic architecture that was completed in 691-692 CE by Umayyad Caliph Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan (Grabar 2006, 45; Rabbat 1989). The building is erected over the holy Foundation Rock (the Rock), a place of worship in Abrahamic faiths (Levy-Rubin 2017).

It is an octagonal structure with a great gilded dome measuring about 20 meters in diameter at the top of which is a drum and an inner circle of piers and columns (Milwright 2016, 112). The outside is decorated with bright blue, turquoise and gold tiles and everything inside is beautifully decorated with marble, mosaic patterns with vegetal and geometry and the Quranic inscriptions that highlight the oneness of God (tawhid) (Grabar 2006; Milwright 2016). The design is based on the Byzantine tradition with innovations based on the principles of Islamic art, which is not based on figurative images (Rabbat 1989).

The Dome of the Rock is not a mosque for regular congregational prayer, but rather a commemorative shrine, which is located on a high platform in the Haram al-Sharif (Grabar 2006). It is the location where, according to Islamic tradition, Prophet Muhammad rose to heaven during the Night Journey (Isra and Mi’raj) (Grabar 2006). The rock in the center of the site is also of great importance to Jewish and Christian beliefs, and it is associated with the near-sacrifice of Isaac by Abraham as well as the other prophetic events (Rabbat 1989; Levy-Rubin 2017).

The Dome of the Rock is a great representation of the Islamic heritage, spirituality, and architectural brilliance today, with Muslim pilgrims and visitors worldwide who want to connect with the divine history of this holy site (Rabbat 1989).

Spiritual Benefits of the Site

Pilgrimage to the Dome of the Rock offers spiritual benefits that are closely related to the visual and architectural characteristics. The centre of it is the Foundation Stone, which is thought to have been the place where Prophet Muhammad ascended (Mi’raj) to heaven (Grabar 2006). Being in front of this rock produces an immediate association with the divine experience and makes pilgrims remember the journey of the Prophet and the persistence of the monotheistic religion.

The glossy golden dome, seen in the distance, is an image of the heavens and it raises the spirit to God (Rabbat 1989). It is a shining structure, paved with detailed tiles, which give a sense of divine light, and awe and humility (Milwright 2016). The octagonal shape symbolizes perfection and rebirth, encourages meditative circumambulation as pilgrims approach the building creating inner peace and spiritual renewal (Rabbat 1989). The ambulatory surrounding the dome gives the feeling of intimacy with the divine, similar to the heavenly ascension of the Prophet (Grabar 2006).

There are no human or animal figure in the mosaics inside, which directs attention solely to God (Rabbat 1989). Intricate vegetal designs and Quranic texts (with a focus on tawhid) cleanse the mind and strengthen the faith (Milwright 2016). light cast through stained-glass windows fills the sacred space with colors that remind of paradise, give comfort, forgive sins, and increase the bond with Allah (Milwright 2016).

To Muslims, the spiritual rewards are multiplied by praying or contemplating here. The architecture transforms the visitor’s experience, shifting focus from earthly concerns to a sense of the divine, the architecture provides peace, enlightenment and a new devotion to faith. A lot of pilgrims claim that they felt very intimate with God and the Prophet and that the Dome of the Rock is a spiritually rejuvenating place.
